Hogarth · Enterprise Client (Anonymized) · 2024–2025

Building a Global Content Governance System from the Ground Up

Content operations · Framework design · Cross-functional leadership · 100+ global locales

The situation

A global content engine with no rulebook

When I joined the engagement at Hogarth, our enterprise client was managing content across more than 100 global markets — but without a unified system for doing it. Each locale operated with its own interpretation of brand standards. Approval workflows differed by region. Compliance checkpoints were inconsistently applied. And because there was no shared language for how content decisions got made, every ambiguous situation escalated — to me, to the client, to whoever happened to be in the room.

The output was inconsistent. The process was slow. And the team was spending significant time on decisions that a well-designed system should have made automatically.

"The problem wasn't effort — the team was working hard. The problem was that there was no framework for that effort to flow through."

My role

Architect and implementer — not just executor

I owned this initiative end-to-end. That meant auditing the existing state across markets, identifying where the gaps were creating the most friction, designing the governance model, building the documentation and toolkits, and then leading the organizational work of getting distributed teams to actually adopt it.

I was not handed a framework and asked to roll it out. I built it from a blank page.

The approach

Design for the edge cases, not just the easy ones

I started by mapping where decisions were actually getting stuck — not where people thought they were getting stuck, but where the real friction lived in the day-to-day workflow. I interviewed content leads across regions, reviewed audit trails in the CMS, and catalogued recurring escalation patterns.

What I found was that most inconsistencies traced back to three root causes: unclear ownership at the handoff between global and regional teams, no defined compliance checkpoints for regulated content categories, and templates that left too much open to interpretation.

The governance model I designed addressed each of these directly. It established clear ownership structures for every stage of the content lifecycle, defined mandatory compliance checkpoints with specific criteria — not vague guidance — and introduced a tiered template system that gave regional teams creative flexibility within tightly defined guardrails.

Critically, the model was documented in a way that made it self-service. The goal was that a new team member in any market could read the framework, understand how to apply it, and make the right call without needing to ask me.

The outcomes

Fewer escalations. Faster cycles. Consistent output.

Escalation reduction

Recurring cross-regional compliance flags dropped significantly once ownership and checkpoint criteria were clearly defined

Onboarding speed

New market onboarding time shortened as teams could orient themselves using the framework rather than relying on institutional knowledge

Review cycle clarity

Content review rounds became more purposeful — fewer rounds spent on structural issues that governance now prevented upstream

Team confidence

Regional leads reported higher confidence in making autonomous content decisions within the defined framework

Diagnosing and Fixing a Broken Content Production Workflow

Process improvement · Bottleneck analysis · Workflow redesign · Cross-team coordination

The situation

A bottleneck nobody had named — but everyone felt

Midway through the Hogarth engagement, a pattern became impossible to ignore: content was consistently arriving late to the localization team, which was creating a downstream cascade that pushed regional publication dates and frustrated client stakeholders. The issue was being treated as a capacity problem — the assumption was that the team simply needed more bandwidth.

I suspected the real problem was structural. So I took a closer look.

"It wasn't a people problem. It was a handoff problem — and handoff problems are design problems."

My role

Diagnosis, design, and implementation

I led the full process improvement initiative — from root cause analysis through stakeholder alignment to workflow redesign and adoption. I was not asked to do this; I identified the need, proposed the investigation, and owned the outcome.

The approach

Follow the work, not the assumptions

I mapped the actual content workflow from brief intake through final regional publication — not the documented process, but what was actually happening in practice. I tracked where content sat idle between stages, how long each handoff took, and what triggered the delays.

What I found was a single structural flaw: the brief stage was consistently delivering content to the localization team in a format that required significant reformatting before translation could begin. Localization teams were spending the first portion of every project doing cleanup work that should have been done upstream — and because that wasn't visible to the production team, nobody had ever connected the cause to the effect.

The fix was two-pronged. First, I redesigned the content brief template to enforce source formatting requirements before anything left production — making the correct format the path of least resistance. Second, I introduced a lightweight pre-handoff checklist that production leads completed before passing content forward, creating a visible quality gate that hadn't existed before.

I brought the localization team into the design process from the start, which both improved the solution and made adoption significantly easier — they had shaped it, so they trusted it.

The outcomes

A structural fix with compounding benefits

Handoff quality

Reformatting delays at the localization stage were eliminated, recovering meaningful time in each content cycle

Downstream reliability

Regional publication dates became more predictable as the upstream fix stabilized the full pipeline

Client confidence

Stakeholder visibility into delivery timelines improved as the process became more consistent and reportable

Team adoption

Cross-team buy-in was high because both production and localization teams were involved in designing the solution

SoFi Technologies · B2B Financial Product · 2022–2024

Leading Web Content Strategy for a High-Stakes B2B Product Launch

Product launch · Web content strategy · Cross-functional leadership · SEO · Deadline pressure

The situation

A compressed timeline, a complex product, multiple audiences

At SoFi, product launches moved fast — and the web content had to move faster. For one of the more complex B2B product launches I led, the challenge wasn't just volume (landing pages, partner pages, campaign content, SEO copy) — it was that the product served meaningfully different audiences with different levels of sophistication, different questions, and different conversion triggers.

The timeline was tight. The stakeholder list was long. And the content had to be right — in a regulated financial environment, accuracy isn't optional.

"The hardest part wasn't the writing. It was holding the strategic thread across six teams with six different definitions of done."

My role

Content strategy lead and cross-functional coordinator

I owned the web content strategy for the launch end-to-end — from defining the content architecture and user journey across audience segments, through writing and editing, through cross-functional review, through deployment and QA. I was the connective tissue between Product Marketing, Design, Engineering, Legal, and Data Science throughout the process.

The approach

Audience clarity first, content second

Before writing a single word, I pushed the team to get explicit about who we were talking to at each stage of the web experience — something that sounds obvious but rarely gets done rigorously enough in practice. I facilitated a working session with Product Marketing to map the distinct audience segments (financial institution partners, enterprise procurement, end-user decision-makers) against their specific questions, objections, and trust signals at each stage of the journey.

That audience map became the strategic foundation for all content decisions: what went on which page, in what order, at what depth. It also gave the team a shared reference point for the inevitable disagreements about what the content should say — instead of arguing opinions, we argued audience needs, which is a much more productive conversation.

I then built the content architecture — page hierarchy, content types, SEO targets — and began drafting in parallel, using a staged review process with Legal and Compliance that I had pre-negotiated to avoid the eleventh-hour bottlenecks that had derailed previous launches.

When a significant scope change landed three weeks before launch — a new audience segment added to the brief — I restructured the content plan, re-prioritized the production queue, and kept the team moving without losing the strategic coherence of the overall experience.

The outcomes

On time, on strategy, measurably effective

Launch delivery

Full web content suite delivered on schedule despite a mid-project scope expansion adding a third audience segment

SEO performance

Target keyword pages indexed and began ranking within the first month; SEO strategy informed by audience research performed above baseline

Partner engagement

Partner-facing pages supported a campaign that drove meaningful inbound interest from target B2B segments in the launch quarter

Process improvement

Pre-negotiated Legal review process reduced last-minute content changes and became a template for subsequent launches
